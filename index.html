OPEN EDUCATIONAL OBSERVATORY

What is it actually like to observe at a research telescope? Open the dome, slew to a target, center it on the slit, watch photons accumulate, and reduce your own data — free, browser-based astronomy labs built around the real workflow of an observing run.

No installation, no accounts. Works on any modern computer, in the classroom or at home.

Why we built this

Astronomy is an observational science, but most students never get the chance to sit at the controls of a research telescope. These activities aim to close that gap with an uncompromising focus on realism: the dome must be opened, tracking must be switched on (or the sky will drift out from under you), long slews land close to — but rarely exactly on — the target, and signal-to-noise is earned one Poisson-distributed photon at a time. The frustrations are gentle, but they are the authentic frustrations, and working through them is where the learning lives.

The data are real, too, wherever possible: the spectral-classification activity draws its standards from a published library of observed stellar spectra, so the features students learn to read are the features the sky actually shows.

Lineage & credit

We are far from the first to pursue this vision. For roughly three decades, Project CLEA (Contemporary Laboratory Experiences in Astronomy) at Gettysburg College — and in particular VIREO, the Virtual Educational Observatory, by Glenn Snyder and Laurence Marschall — introduced students worldwide to realistic simulated observing. Several of our design choices, from the telescope control panel to the photon-counting spectrometer display and the standard/unknown/difference classification workflow, are consciously influenced by that earlier work, and this project is offered partly as an homage to it.

To be clear: this project was not produced by, and is not affiliated with or endorsed by, Project CLEA, its authors, or Gettysburg College — and it contains none of their code. Reviving and modernizing these laboratory experiences is an ongoing effort that will grow over time; activities will be added, revised, and occasionally rethought as the science itself moves.
