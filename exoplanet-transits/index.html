Information

What this is

This activity simulates CCD time-series photometry of transiting exoplanets: observing a known planet host through a filter for several hours, measuring its brightness against comparison stars in the same field, and fitting the resulting light curve to recover the transit's depth, duration, and mid-transit time — and from the depth, the radius of the planet. It runs in any modern browser with no installation.

The exercise, briefly

1. Choose a target from the transit program, open the dome, and slew to it (tracking engages on arrival). The Ephemeris Service then predicts tonight's ingress, mid-transit, and egress for that field, and recommends a sequence length. 2. Take a test image and check the counts table: nothing may saturate, and you want the smallest σ per exposure you can get — that usually means the longest exposure that stays safely below the full well. Bright hosts force short exposures. 3. Turn on Label stars to identify the target and the catalog's suggested comparison stars, then place three apertures: press Target, Comp 1, or Comp 2 and then click that star on the image (press the button again to place the next one; Clear apertures starts over). Comparison stars near the target's brightness give the least noise; stars marked VAR are known variables and will corrupt your light curve; unmarked stars are uncharted. 4. Turn on the autoguider, confirm the sequence length covers baseline on both sides of the transit, and start. 5. On the Fit the Transit page, slide the trapezoid model onto your data — mid-transit and depth first, then duration and baseline — aiming for a reduced χ² near 1 and residuals that are pure scatter. Save each fit to the results log, then compare your planet radius, duration, and mid-transit time with the published values in the NASA Exoplanet Archive.

The physics you are using

A transit is an eclipse: the planet blocks the fraction of the star's disk that it covers, so the depth of the light curve is the ratio of areas, depth = (Rp/R★)². Measuring a depth therefore measures a planet's size — but only relative to its star, which is why the stellar radius R★ (from spectroscopy plus the Gaia parallax — someone else's careful work) must be supplied from the catalog before your fit becomes a radius in physical units. The duration and the shape of ingress and egress carry information about the orbit's geometry, and the mid-transit time is what refines the ephemeris: transits arriving early or late are how astronomers detect additional planets and other subtleties. Nothing in the light curve gives the planet's mass — that requires radial velocities, a different measurement entirely.

What is and isn't physical

The six program targets are real transiting exoplanets: their coordinates, magnitudes, colors, orbital periods, transit depths, durations, and stellar radii follow published values (NASA Exoplanet Archive and the discovery and follow-up literature) to within their uncertainties. What the simulation schedules is the clock time of tonight's transit, so that every observing session has an event to catch; a real observer would consult a transit prediction service the same way, but on nature's schedule rather than the classroom's. The predicted mid-time is also deliberately imperfect by up to about ±10 minutes, so measuring when the transit actually arrives is a real measurement rather than a lookup.

Photon counting is genuinely Poisson: rates scale with stellar magnitude, filter, and exposure time, and bright stars saturate the detector in long exposures — which is why the test image exists. Error bars follow full propagation through the target and the comparison-star counts, so a faint comparison star genuinely adds noise to every point, and the σ column in the aperture table shows this before you commit to hours of observing. Some field stars are intrinsically variable: the catalog flags the known ones, and the anonymous ones are a gamble — choosing comparison stars is part of the measurement, exactly as in real transit follow-up. Differential photometry against those comparison stars removes most atmospheric variation; what survives is scintillation (falling as √exposure), a slow systematic drift of a fraction of a millimagnitude, and an irreducible floor. Each exposure carries an 8 s readout, and images taken with tracking off are trailed and useless. Detector images are displayed in grayscale, as astronomical data really are; the finder TV keeps its color view. Time uses real UT/JD/LST for a Kitt Peak–like site.

Time compression. Transit photometry means staring at one field for hours, so during an observing sequence the observatory clock — UT, LST, and JD together — runs at a uniform 15× rate: about four real minutes per simulated hour, capped at 5 simulated hours (about 20 real minutes) per run. The photometry is computed per simulated second, so measured depths, durations, and mid-times are unaffected by the compression. The sequence clock is anchored to the wall clock, so a run continues correctly if you switch browser tabs while the photons accumulate. Horizon and airmass limits are deliberately not enforced, so any target can be observed at any hour.

The main simplification is the transit shape itself. The model here is a trapezoid — flat baseline, linear ingress, flat bottom, linear egress — and the fitting tool holds its ingress and egress ramps at 20% of the duration. Real transits are rounded by stellar limb darkening, which also makes them slightly deeper and rounder toward the blue; the depth here is treated as the same in every filter. The synthetic light curves are generated with each planet's own ingress fraction, which is generally not 20%, so a perfect trapezoid fit still leaves a small structured residual near ingress and egress and a reduced χ² somewhat above 1. This is honest about the tool rather than the sky: a trapezoid is the standard first-pass model, and research-grade work uses a limb-darkened model (Mandel & Agol 2002) fitted by least squares or MCMC. Nothing in this simulation is a source of research measurements.

The session report

The Fit the Transit page can print a report of your work (one page per saved fit, plus a summary page) through your browser's own print dialog — choose Save as PDF as the destination to get a file you can attach to your written submission. The button becomes available once you have saved at least one fit to the results log.

Each report carries a short session ID and the real-world UT date and time at which the session began and the report was printed. These identify one particular observing session. Note that these real-UT stamps are not the same clock as the UT times reported for the observations themselves: those are the simulation's own observatory clock, which schedules your transit and runs compressed during a sequence, so the two are not expected to agree. Both appear on the report deliberately — the simulated times are the astronomically meaningful ones for your measurements, while the real-UT stamps record when the work was done. No personal information is collected, stored, or transmitted by this activity; the session ID is simply a random label generated in your browser.

One further consequence of observing being a measurement rather than a lookup: the depth of each program transit carries a small offset, fixed for your session and well inside the published uncertainty. Your fitted planet radius should agree with the literature value, but your numbers are your own, from your own data.

Accessibility

This activity aims to conform to WCAG 2.1 Level AA. All measurements, readouts, tables, and equations are real HTML text available to screen readers and browser zoom; every canvas carries a text alternative that points to where the same values appear as text, and the telescope and camera status lines announce their updates, with the numbers a non-visual user needs written into the message. Every control is keyboard-operable. The telescope's direction pad works from the keyboard: focus a direction button and hold Enter or Space to slew. Placing photometry apertures — normally done by clicking a star on the CCD image — has a full keyboard alternative: press Target, Comp 1, or Comp 2, then move keyboard focus onto the CCD image (Tab to it) and use the arrow keys to move a highlight between stars and Enter to place the aperture on the highlighted star. The image announces the highlighted star and its role (target, suggested comparison, or known variable) as you move.

An accessibility mode (toggle below the page header; enabled automatically if your system requests increased contrast) brightens the interface, restyles every chart and image overlay in a colorblind-safe high-contrast palette, and adds redundant non-color encodings — aperture markers differ by shape and label as well as color (a filled diamond for the target, an open circle for a comparison, a cross for a known variable), and the two comparison apertures draw as dashed rings against the target's solid ring. Accessibility mode also reveals an Assist: place apertures button that assigns all three apertures at once — the target to the program host and the two comparisons to suitable, similar-brightness catalog stars — for anyone, low-vision, motor-impaired, or otherwise, for whom placing three apertures by fine pointing is the barrier. Using it leaves no mark in the session report. The observing task is intrinsically visual in places; if any part of this activity presents a barrier these accommodations don't resolve, students should contact their instructor, and issues can be reported through the project repository.

Credit & lineage

The aim of this project is to give students a realistic experience of controlling a telescope and analyzing their own data, grounded in the working practices of professional astronomy. The workflow here — pointing at a known host star, observing it for hours against comparison stars in the same field, and fitting the light curve for depth, duration, and mid-transit time — is standard astronomical practice, used essentially unchanged by the professional and amateur observers who follow up transiting planets today.
