What this is

This activity simulates CCD aperture photometry of open star clusters in Johnson–Cousins UBVRI filters, and the analysis of the resulting Hertzsprung–Russell (color–magnitude) diagrams: fitting a zero-age main sequence to measure distance and reddening, and fitting theoretical isochrones to measure age. It runs in any modern browser with no installation.

The exercise, briefly

1. Choose a Target from the listed star clusters, open the dome, and slew to the cluster (tracking engages on arrival). 2. Take a preview image to check framing, then integrate through a filter; the camera returns the averaged image of the exposure stack. 3. On the image, place the aperture on a blank patch and Set sky, then switch to Star readings: click each program star, nudge the aperture onto its center, and Record. Measure through B and V (and U, R, I if assigned), aiming for S/N ≳ 100 — about 10,000 net counts for 1% (0.01 mag) photometry. The CCD field is smaller than the cluster, so offset the telescope and take more images to reach outer members. 4. Once enough stars have both a B and a V measurement (25 by default; instructors can adjust with ?nreq=N in the URL), send them to the HR Diagram page, plot your measurements, fit the ZAMS with the distance-modulus and reddening sliders, and fit an isochrone with the age slider. If you measured U as well, the Two-Color diagram (U−B vs B−V) lets you determine E(B−V) independently of distance by sliding the intrinsic main-sequence locus along the reddening vector — the classic photometric method — before fitting the CMD. Two example catalogs (NGC 752, IC 4665) are available from the start for practicing the fitting tools — but the program clusters must be measured at the telescope; no stored shortcut exists for them. When your fits are done, save each one and download the session report - a timestamped PDF of your observing log, saved fits, and diagrams, carrying a unique session code and this session’s photon noise, suitable for turning in with your write-up. 5. Convert your distance modulus to a distance: log D = (V−MV)/5 + 1, ideally after correcting for extinction AV = 3.1·E(B−V).

What is and isn't physical

Photon counting is genuinely Poisson and is frozen into each image at capture, like real data: S/N grows as √(exposure time), rates scale with stellar magnitude, and sky background is measured from the frame and subtracted, as in real aperture photometry. Bright stars saturate the detector in long exposures and must be retaken with short ones. Miscentered apertures lose flux; stars closer than about an aperture diameter blend, and a simple aperture cannot separate them. A sky aperture accidentally containing a faint star silently contaminates the sky value — exactly as at a real telescope. Images taken with tracking off are trailed and useless for photometry. The cursor readout reports counts in the detector pixel (1.5″ pixels) under the mouse — the count peak marks a star's center, which is how astronomers really centroid. Long exposures play accelerated (~5 s of wall time each) as a concession to class time, and horizon/airmass limits are deliberately not enforced so any cluster can be observed at any hour. Detector images are displayed in grayscale, as astronomical data really are; the finder TV keeps its color view. Time uses real UT/JD/LST for a Kitt Peak–like site. Cluster stars are synthetic: members are generated from the same isochrone family used by the fitting tool, at published values of distance modulus, reddening, and age, with a realistic mass distribution, an unresolved-binary sequence, and a few non-member contaminants. Fits therefore recover literature-like answers, but nothing here is a source of research measurements. Model details are in the provenance section below.

Model provenance

The isochrones and ZAMS in this release are a parametric family built for this activity, not output of a stellar-evolution code. The ZAMS follows standard Allen's Astrophysical Quantities–style calibrations and the U−B/B−V dwarf locus is the classic Johnson relation; the turnoff-color–age mapping is anchored to published cluster ages (Pleiades ≈ 100 Myr, Praesepe ≈ 700 Myr, M67 ≈ 4 Gyr); the turnoff hook, subgiant and giant branches, metallicity shifts, and V−R/V−I colors are simplified constructions. Because the synthetic clusters are generated from this same family, fits recover the published input values by design — adequate and honest for the pedagogy, but not a validation of the models. An upgrade to real MIST isochrones (Choi et al. 2016; Dotter 2016) is planned; the export tooling for it already exists in the repository.

Credit & lineage

The aim of this project is to give students a realistic experience of controlling a telescope and analyzing their own data, grounded in the working practices of professional astronomy. The workflow here — imaging a cluster field, extracting aperture photometry, and fitting the color–magnitude diagram for distance, reddening, and age — is standard astronomical practice, used essentially unchanged at observatories everywhere.

In bringing that practice into the classroom we gratefully acknowledge Project CLEA (Contemporary Laboratory Experiences in Astronomy, Gettysburg College) and VIREO — The Virtual Educational Observatory by Glenn Snyder and Laurence Marschall, whose exercises Photoelectric Photometry of the Pleiades and HR Diagrams of Star Clusters demonstrated decades ago how effective simulated observing can be, and which we consulted while designing this activity.
