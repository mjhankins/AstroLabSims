What this is

This activity simulates measuring the redshifts and distances of galaxies with an optical telescope and photon-counting spectrometer, recreating — with modern tools — the experiment through which Edwin Hubble and Milton Humason revealed the expanding Universe. Students build their own velocity–distance diagram, fit the Hubble parameter, and estimate the age of the Universe. It runs in any modern browser with no installation.

The exercise, briefly

1. Choose a cluster field from the target list — the default Hubble catalog holds the six clusters of the classic Hubble–Humason program (Virgo out to Hydra); the full catalog adds six more of Humason's survey fields, including spiral-rich Hercules. Open the dome and slew; tracking engages on arrival. 2. In the Telescope view, center a galaxy's nucleus between the slit jaws and integrate. Aim for S/N ≳ 10: bright Virgo members take seconds, the faintest Hydra members over a minute — patience the sky really does demand. Save the spectrum. 3. On the Measure Redshift page, first classify the spectrum: elliptical (absorption lines on a red continuum with a 4000 Å break) or star-forming (emission spikes on a blue continuum). Measure the Ca K & H line centers directly with the Line measurement tool, or select the matching template and slide the redshift until every marker sits in a line at once — the marker pattern stretches as it slides, because lines move to λrest × (1+z). Distance comes from the apparent magnitude via the distance modulus, assuming M = −22 for a bright cluster galaxy. Record at least one galaxy per field. 4. On the Hubble Diagram page, fit a line through the origin to your points; its slope is H₀, and T = 1/H₀ ≈ 978/H₀ Gyr estimates the expansion age.

What is and isn't physical

Photon counting is genuinely Poisson: S/N grows as √(integration time), count rates scale with apparent magnitude, and every exposure includes sky background with zero-redshift [O I] airglow lines at 5577 and 6300 Å — emission from Earth's own atmosphere, exactly as at a real telescope. One deliberate concession: the count-rate–magnitude scaling is mildly compressed relative to strict flux scaling, so bright fields take seconds rather than milliseconds while faint fields still cost real minutes. Placing the slit on a galaxy's outskirts instead of its nucleus reduces the count rate; with tracking off the sky drifts west at the sidereal rate and the target slides out of the slit mid-integration. Field-list slews land close to — but rarely exactly on — the target, with a per-field pointing error that repeats within a session, as a real pointing model would. A point-like object in the slit may turn out to be a foreground Milky Way star, whose lines show no redshift — a trap the sky sets for real surveys too.

The cluster fields are named for, and placed near, the historic Hubble–Humason survey clusters, with recession velocities close to the classical values — but every galaxy, spectrum, and magnitude is generated by the simulation around a built-in expansion rate. Galaxies carry realistic peculiar velocities within their clusters and ±0.25 mag of standard-candle scatter about the assumed M = −22, so student Hubble diagrams show honest scatter about the trend; fitting the trend rather than any single point is part of the lesson. Elliptical members have absorption-line spectra with a 4000 Å break; star-forming members show nebular emission ([O II], Hβ, [O III], Hα) and render as spiral disks when resolvable — beyond z ≈ 0.066, Hα slides past the red end of the spectrometer, which is the authentic reason real surveys lean on [O II] and Ca K/H at large distances. The smallest galaxies are drawn slightly larger than strict scaling for visibility. Velocities use the classical Doppler formula v = cz throughout, as in the historical exercise; the relativistic correction at the largest redshifts here (z ≈ 0.2) is real but knowingly ignored. The simulated sky is deterministic — every student sees the same galaxies at the same positions — but each browser session adds small observational "calibration" noise (a few hundredths of a magnitude in photometry, a few tens of km/s in velocity), so measured values differ slightly from session to session, as real observing nights do. A timestamped session report (PDF) of the observing log, data table, and fitted diagram can be downloaded from the Hubble Diagram page for submission alongside written work. Nothing in this simulation is a source of research measurements.

Accessibility

This activity aims to conform to WCAG 2.1 Level AA. All measurements, readouts, tables, and equations are real HTML text available to screen readers and browser zoom; canvas graphics carry text alternatives, and the spectrometer status line announces its updates. Every control is keyboard-operable, including the telescope: focus a direction button and hold Enter or Space to slew. The crosshair measurement has a full keyboard alternative — wavelengths and redshifts can be typed directly into the measurement boxes. An accessibility mode (toggle below the page header; enabled automatically if your system requests increased contrast) brightens the interface, restyles all chart overlays in a colorblind-safe high-contrast palette, and adds redundant non-color encodings — the K and H markers, for instance, differ by solid versus dashed line as well as by color. Enabling accessibility mode also reveals a centering assist button on the Observatory page that places the spectrometer slit on a galaxy in the current field, cycling to the next member with each press so every galaxy is reachable — for anyone, low-vision, motor-impaired, or otherwise, for whom fine slewing is a barrier; using it leaves no mark in the session report. The observing task is intrinsically visual in places; if any part of this activity presents a barrier these accommodations don't resolve, students should contact their instructor, and issues can be reported through the project repository.

Credit & lineage

The aim of this project is to give students a realistic experience of controlling a telescope and analyzing their own data, grounded in the working practices of professional astronomy. Measuring galaxy redshifts from spectra — by line identification or template fitting — and building the redshift–distance relation is standard astronomical practice, carried out essentially unchanged from Hubble's day to today's large surveys.

In bringing that practice into the classroom we gratefully acknowledge Project CLEA (Contemporary Laboratory Experiences in Astronomy, Gettysburg College) and VIREO — The Virtual Educational Observatory by Glenn Snyder and Laurence Marschall, whose exercise The Hubble Redshift Distance Relation demonstrated decades ago how effective simulated observing can be, and which we consulted while designing this activity.
