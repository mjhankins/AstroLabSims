First, classify: is this an elliptical spectrum (absorption lines on a red continuum with a strong break near 4000 Å) or a star-forming one (emission spikes on a blue continuum)? For ellipticals, start by measuring the Ca K & H line centers yourself with the Line measurement tool. The Template fit tool works for both kinds: pick the matching template and slide the redshift until every marker sits in a line at once — the pattern stretches as it slides, since lines move to λrest × (1+z).

The dashed gray markers never move: the K/H/G rest wavelengths, and the [O I] 5577 Å airglow line — emission from Earth's own atmosphere, which appears at zero redshift in every spectrum you take.

A correct fit puts every marker in a line simultaneously. If only one marker matches, you may have the wrong template — or the wrong line. Beyond z ≈ 0.066, Hα slides past the red end of the spectrometer; this is why real surveys lean on [O II] and Ca K/H at large distances.

Record at least one galaxy from each field, then open the Hubble Diagram tab. Recording two or three galaxies per cluster and comparing them is a good way to see — and beat down — the scatter.

Each point is a galaxy from your data table: recession velocity against distance. Real points scatter — galaxies have their own motions within clusters of hundreds of km/s on top of the cosmic expansion, and the standard-candle assumption (M = −22 for every galaxy) is only approximately true. Fit the trend, not any single point.

Age of the Universe

If every galaxy has always receded at its present speed, then the time since they were all together is T = D / v = 1 / H₀. Converting units (1 Mpc = 3.09 × 10¹⁹ km; 3.15 × 10⁷ s per year):

T = 1 / H₀ ≈ 978 / H₀  [Gyr, with H₀ in km/s/Mpc]

More about this simulation

All galaxies, spectra, and photometry here are simulated for instruction — cluster names and recession velocities follow the historic Hubble–Humason survey fields, but nothing in this activity is a source of research measurements. Velocities use the classical Doppler formula v = cz; at the largest redshifts here (z ≈ 0.2) the relativistic correction is real but is ignored for pedagogy.
